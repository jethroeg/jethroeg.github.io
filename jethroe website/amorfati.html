Amor Fati: Love of one's fate

My formula for greatness in a human being is amor fati: that one wants nothing to be different, not forward, not backward, not in all eternity. Not merely bear what is necessary, still less conceal it… but love it.

—Friedrich Nietzsche

We torture ourselves. Yes, we are always thinking about the things that we cannot take back. We are blaming ourselves for not being the person that we want to be. It is part of human nature to care about what other people think and that puts a burden to how we live our lives. In a life that is dedicated to the pursuit of excellence, we tend to regret our school grades, not getting that promotion, not getting accepted to our dream college, or being left out in terms of career. For me, that is one of the core human struggles — caring too much about what other people think and blaming ourselves for the things we cannot change. Like, why even care? We are encompassed by regret that we even feel frustrated for little things (yes, I am pertaining to that embarrassing thing you did years ago that will pop up to your mind at 12 midnight that no one even remembers).

For the famous German Philosopher Friedrich Nietzsche, the key to human greatness is Amor Fati, a Latin phrase which translates as 'a love of one's fate'. This is the total acceptance of everything that happened and will happen in our lives. Total acceptance of how things went and how it will go. A feeling where you surrender yourself to your own fate with full acceptance that if you were to relive your life, you wouldn't want to change anything. This is what's missing in most of our lives. We are existing but not truly living. We forget about the simple things in life that we should be thankful for and we live in constant restlessness. Life should be a beautiful adventure. We forgot to enjoy the simple indulgence of life and let fate work on its own.

—Jethroe Gatbunton
